\begin{center}
{\LARGE \textbf{AIQ: The Human Capability to Harness AI}}\\[0.35em]
{\large A one-page concept note for grant development and team formation}
\end{center}

\textbf{Why now.}
AI systems are already strong enough to transform how people work, learn, and make decisions. Yet the value they create does not depend on model capability alone. Recent attention has focused on prompt engineering, context engineering, and harness engineering: how to instruct models, structure context, and build systems around them. We argue that an equally important variable remains underdeveloped: the \textbf{human capability} to use AI well. We call this capability \textbf{AIQ} -- Artificial Intelligence Quotient.

\textbf{What is AIQ.}
AIQ is a person's ability to understand where AI is strong, where it is weak, and how to design productive human--AI workflows around that moving boundary. In practice, high AIQ means knowing which parts of a workflow can genuinely benefit from AI, what context the model needs, where failure is likely, and where verification or human judgment must remain in the loop. AIQ is therefore not just about writing better prompts. It is about combining knowledge of one's own work with knowledge of AI's jagged, shifting capability frontier.

\textbf{Why it matters.}
AI is powerful, but uneven. It can solve difficult tasks surprisingly well, then fail on nearby tasks that appear simple to humans. It also often produces plausible outputs without clearly signaling uncertainty. This makes misuse easy to miss. People with low AIQ may over-trust AI, underuse it, or deploy it in the wrong parts of a workflow. People with high AIQ can instead turn AI into reliable augmentation: faster research, better drafting, stronger analysis, more thoughtful iteration, and improved quality control. As AI adoption accelerates, the difference between low and high AIQ may become a major driver of productivity, safety, and inclusion.

\textbf{Our proposal.}
We want to develop AIQ as both a research concept and a practical intervention. An initial pilot project could:
\begin{itemize}
\item define a clear conceptual framework for AIQ, grounded in real human--AI workflows;
\item identify core dimensions such as task selection, context provision, failure anticipation, verification, and ethical use;
\item prototype a simple AIQ assessment or rubric that can evolve as models change;
\item test the framework in academic and professional settings where responsible AI use has high stakes.
\end{itemize}
This would create a bridge between technical AI progress and human capability development. It would also generate a practical foundation for training, evaluation, and policy conversations about how society can harness AI responsibly.

\textbf{Why this is a strong grant idea.}
AIQ addresses a global challenge that cuts across education, work, health, public institutions, and innovation: how to ensure that increasingly powerful AI benefits people who know how to use it wisely, rather than amplifying error, inequality, or hype. The project is interdisciplinary by design and well suited to a small, ambitious pilot: it can produce a framework, an early measurement tool, case studies, and a basis for follow-on funding.

\textbf{Call for collaborators.}
We are building an interdisciplinary team to shape this idea into a pilot project and grant application. We would especially welcome collaborators with backgrounds in AI, education, psychology, HCI, organizational behavior, public policy, ethics, design, and domain-specific workflow research. The ambition is not only to study AI systems, but to understand and improve the human ability to harness them well.
